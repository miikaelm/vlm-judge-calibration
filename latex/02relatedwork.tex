\section{Text-in-Image Editing}

\label{Text-in-Image Editing}

\subsection{Content Editing}

Changing what text says in an image, while preserving the surrounding visual context, has received substantial research attention and has seen meaningful progress. AnyText \cite{tuo2024anytext} introduced a diffusion-based pipeline with an auxiliary text-control module that uses text glyph images to guide generation, achieving strong results on multilingual text rendering and replacement. TextCtrl \cite{zeng2024textctrl} addresses scene text editing by disentangling style and glyph structure priors, enabling high-fidelity content replacement. Commercial systems such as Gemini's image editing capabilities \cite{google2025nanobanana} have further pushed the frontier. The common thread across this line of work is that content editing is relatively well-posed: the target is semantically defined (change word A to word B), and success can be assessed by reading the output with OCR.

\subsection{Style and Quantitative Editing}

Style editing, changing how text looks rather than what it says, presents a different class of challenges. Quantitative style edits, where the desired change is numerically specified (scale by 20\%, move 40 pixels, change weight to bold), are particularly demanding because success requires not just plausible visual quality but precise numeric correspondence to the instruction.

STELLAR \cite{seo2025stellar} identifies an evaluation gap in this space: standard metrics fail to capture whether visual style has been preserved after an edit. Their Text Appearance Similarity (TAS) metric uses a trained style encoder to independently measure font, color, and background similarity, enabling reference-free style evaluation. This directly motivates fine-grained judge evaluation: a model that correctly resizes text but inadvertently alters its font has failed in a way that aggregate metrics conceal.

TextEditBench \cite{gui2025texteditbench} is the most comprehensive benchmark for quantitative text editing to date. It covers content modification, spatial transformation (repositioning and scaling), and visual consistency, documenting that current state-of-the-art models struggle significantly with the spatial and quantitative dimensions. WeEdit \cite{zhang2026weedit} approaches the same problem from a dataset construction angle, providing a large-scale benchmark of text-in-image editing tasks with VLM-based evaluation. These two benchmarks jointly establish the state of the field: quantitative style editing is an open problem, and the evaluation framework used to measure progress on it relies on VLM judges.

The commercial significance of this problem is substantial. Generative editing models are increasingly positioned as replacements for manual design tools. For this to work in practice, a model must not only execute the requested change but avoid corrupting other visual properties that were not part of the instruction. An edit that changes font weight alongside a requested color change, or shifts letter spacing while scaling text, is a product failure regardless of how good the output looks subjectively. This constraint motivates careful measurement of what current models, and their judges, can and cannot detect.


\section{VLM-as-Judge Evaluation}

\label{VLM-as-Judge Evaluation}

\subsection{The Paradigm and Its Adoption}

The VLM-as-judge paradigm applies a capable VLM as an automated rater of other models' outputs. In text-in-image editing, the judge receives the source image, edit instruction, and edited result, and scores the edit on a rubric covering dimensions such as instruction following, text accuracy, visual consistency, and layout preservation \cite{gui2025texteditbench, zhang2026weedit}. % [FIND REF: REDEdit-Bench] The practical appeal is clear: human annotation at the scale required for benchmark evaluation is expensive and slow, while VLM judges can evaluate large corpora cheaply and reproducibly.

This paradigm has been adopted widely and quickly, often without explicit validation of the judge's reliability on the task at hand. TextEditBench, WeEdit, and related benchmarks all use VLM judges as the primary evaluation mechanism, and the field has largely treated this as a solved scaffolding problem rather than an open empirical question.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Calibration in Text}

In the NLP domain, the reliability of LLM judges has been studied extensively. % [FIND REF: Zheng et al. 2023 MT-Bench / Judging LLM-as-a-Judge]
Zheng et al. introduced MT-Bench and demonstrated that strong LLMs can serve as judges for open-ended text quality, but also documented systematic biases including positional preference (favoring responses that appear first) and self-preference (favoring outputs of the same model family). % [FIND REF: Pandalm, AlpacaEval, additional calibration papers]
Subsequent work has shown that LLM judges are sensitive to prompt framing, can be manipulated by verbosity, and often exhibit poor calibration on fine-grained quality distinctions. % [FIND REF: Shen et al. or similar on LLM judge biases]

These findings motivate analogous scrutiny of VLM judges, but the translation is not straightforward. Judging text quality is primarily semantic; judging image editing quality requires visual perception of specific properties, such as color accuracy, spatial precision, and typographic fidelity. The relevant failure modes are perceptual rather than semantic, and the tools for measuring them (psychometric sensitivity curves, just-noticeable-difference thresholds) come from a different methodological tradition.

\subsection{VLM Perceptual Capabilities and Limitations}

Vision-language models have documented limitations in fine-grained visual perception that are directly relevant to judging text-in-image edits. Counting, spatial reasoning, and attribute binding (correctly associating a property with the right object in a scene) are known weak points for current VLMs. % [FIND REF: MMBench, POPE, or similar VLM capability papers]
Typographic properties such as font weight, letter spacing, and precise color values are not salient features in the natural image distributions on which these models are trained, and there is no reason to assume a priori that models will be sensitive to small deviations in these dimensions.

The closest prior work to our study is research on LLM-as-judge calibration in text evaluation, where it has been established that judge scores may not monotonically track ground-truth quality. Our work extends this line of inquiry to the visual modality and to the specific domain of text style editing, where the error dimensions are perceptual and typographic rather than semantic, and where the stakes for benchmark reliability are correspondingly high.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}

\label{Evaluation Methodology}

\subsection{Benchmark Validity and Diagnostic Evaluation}

Raji et al. \cite{raji2021everything} argue that widely adopted AI benchmarks suffer from construct validity problems: they claim to measure general capabilities but are finite, context-specific artifacts whose composite scores can obscure more than they reveal. Most relevant to our work is their observation that aggregate metrics can mask failures on specific sub-tasks, and their recommendation for diagnostic evaluation that isolates particular failure modes through disaggregated analysis and ablation testing.

TextEditBench is itself subject to this concern. Its composite metrics conflate multiple failure modes, making it difficult to determine whether a low score reflects a failure to execute a spatial transformation, a failure to preserve unmodified visual properties, or a failure to parse the instruction. Our study is designed explicitly as the diagnostic complement to these broad benchmarks: rather than measuring aggregate editing competence, we isolate atomic degradation dimensions and measure judge sensitivity to each independently.

\subsection{Psychometric Methods for Sensitivity Measurement}

The sensitivity analysis in this work is methodologically analogous to psychophysical experiments that estimate perceptual thresholds. The logic is the same: vary stimulus magnitude along a controlled dimension, measure the observer's response, and fit a sensitivity curve to estimate the threshold at which the observer transitions from chance to reliable discrimination.

In sensory psychology, this yields the just-noticeable difference (JND), the smallest magnitude change that is reliably detected. We apply the same framing to VLM judges: at what error magnitude does the judge's score begin to reliably depart from the score given to a correct edit? A judge that is sensitive only at error magnitudes far above human JND is, in a meaningful sense, less reliable than human annotation for those dimensions. This framing provides a principled way to interpret judge performance that goes beyond simple accuracy or correlation metrics.

\subsection{Synthetic Stimuli for Controlled Evaluation}

Several papers in image quality and VLM evaluation have used synthetic or procedurally generated stimuli to achieve precise ground-truth control. % [FIND REF: relevant synthetic stimuli evaluation papers]
The advantage is exactly what motivates our design: when the ground truth is known with machine precision, judge disagreement with the ground truth is unambiguously attributable to judge error rather than ground-truth ambiguity. This is particularly important for calibration studies, where the goal is to characterize the judge rather than to evaluate the editing model.

The approach taken here, browser rendering of HTML/CSS with CSS-level degradation injection, gives pixel-accurate ground truth for all manipulated dimensions, is fully reproducible, and allows systematic coverage of the magnitude space. The trade-off is ecological validity: rendered HTML stimuli differ visually from generative model outputs, which may have stylistic properties (texture, noise, blending artifacts) not present in clean renders. We return to this limitation in Section~\ref{Design Rationale} and in the discussion.
