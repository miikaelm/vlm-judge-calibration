\chapter{Introduction} \label{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

\label{Background and Motivation}

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have rapidly advanced to the point where they can not only describe images but actively manipulate them through natural language instructions \cite{liu2023visualinstructiontuning, openai2024gpt4technicalreport}. This has generated considerable commercial interest: tools that previously required professional expertise in software like Adobe Photoshop can increasingly be operated by describing the desired change in words \cite{adobe2023firefly}. For this shift to be commercially viable, however, the models must not only produce plausible-looking outputs but execute edits with precision. A model asked to make a heading 20\% larger fails the task whether it leaves the size unchanged, misjudges the magnitude, or executes the size change while also altering font weight or family. Both kinds of imprecision, missing the requested edit and introducing unrequested ones, disqualify the model from professional use, where the edited image is itself the deliverable.

Text-in-image editing is one of the most commercially consequential domains for these models: posters, infographics, documents, marketing materials, and user interfaces all rely on text as a primary design element. Research on this domain has converged on a useful distinction between two kinds of edits. \textit{Content editing} changes what text says, replacing one word or phrase with another. This has received substantial attention and is increasingly reliable in both academic models \cite{tuo2024anytext, zeng2024textctrl} and commercial systems. \textit{Style editing} changes how text looks, such as its size, position, weight, color, or typeface, without altering its content. Quantitative style edits, where the target is numerically specified (''scale the title to 150\% of its current size'', ''move the caption 40 pixels to the right''), remain substantially harder.  The distinction is useful but not clean in practice: content edits routinely produce unintended style changes as side effects, where swapping a word also shifts its font, weight, position, or color. 
A judge that misses a font shift in a style edit will miss the same shift when it appears as a content-edit side effect.

To measure progress on quantitative style editing, recent benchmarks have adopted an evaluation paradigm that is practical at scale: a Vision-Language Model (VLM) is presented with the original image, the edit instruction, and the model's output, and asked to score the quality of the edit on a rubric covering dimensions such as instruction following, visual consistency, and layout preservation. TextEditBench \cite{gui2025texteditbench} and WeEdit \cite{zhang2026weedit} represent the current direction of the field, both including style edits among their evaluated operations and both adopting this VLM-as-judge protocol to score model outputs. The appeal is clear: human annotation at scale is expensive and slow, while VLMs can evaluate many examples quickly and at low cost.

The reliability of this protocol depends on what the judge is being asked to discriminate. For text \textit{content}, whether the rendered string matches the requested string, VLMs have a plausible foundation: their OCR capability is well-documented \cite{liu2024ocrbench, fu2025ocrbenchv2}, and the medium for quantitative style editing is typically clean, designed text (posters, infographics, product cards) where OCR is least error-prone. Judging content correctness reduces, roughly, to reading both images and comparing strings, which sits within what VLMs are known to do. For text \textit{style}, whether a requested 20\% scale change landed at 20\%, whether letter spacing shifted by the requested amount, whether font weight changed without altering family, there is no analogous foundation. Fine-grained typographic and spatial discrimination has not been validated for VLM judges, and neither TextEditBench nor WeEdit cites work that establishes it. The protocol is adopted because it is practical at scale, not because it has been shown to work for the discriminations style editing requires. If the judges are blind to certain error types, or only sensitive at error magnitudes far above what constitutes a perceptible failure, benchmark scores built on them provide a false sense of progress.


\section{This Work}

\label{This Work}

This thesis measures the reliability of VLM judges for text-in-image style editing directly. Rather than proposing a new evaluation protocol, we audit the existing one. We construct stimuli where the deviation from a correct edit is known with machine precision, feed them through the same VLM-as-judge pipeline that the field already uses, and observe how judge scores change as a function of error type and magnitude.

The stimulus design uses a browser rendering pipeline (HTML/CSS rendered via headless Chromium) to produce image triples: a source image, a correctly edited ground truth, and a degraded version with a controlled error applied on top of the correct edit. The degradation covers thirteen dimensions spanning typographic properties (font size, font weight, font family, font style, letter spacing, color, opacity), spatial properties (position, rotation), and image-level degradations (blur, noise, JPEG compression). Each dimension is sampled across multiple magnitude tiers, from sub-perceptual to clearly visible, to characterise the full sensitivity curve of each judge.

Three VLMs are evaluated as judges: GPT-4o, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, and Qwen2.5-VL. Each stimulus is evaluated under two experimental conditions. In Experiment 1 (perceptual sensitivity), the judge is shown the ground truth and degraded image and asked whether they differ. In Experiment 2 (edit judgment), the judge is shown the source image, the edit instruction, and the degraded result, replicating the exact benchmark task. The gap between the two experiments measures how much reasoning overhead costs the judge beyond raw visual discrimination.

\section{Research Questions}

\label{Research Questions}

\noindent \textbf{RQ1:} How reliably do VLM judges detect errors in text-in-image edits?

\noindent \textbf{RQ1a:} Does judge reliability vary systematically across error types? Which degradation dimensions constitute blind spots for current VLM judges?

\noindent \textbf{RQ1b:} At what error magnitudes do VLM judges begin to reliably penalise degradations, and how do these thresholds compare to perceptual detectability?

\section{Scope and Boundaries}

\label{Scope and Boundaries}

\textit{Edit types.} This study covers eight edit types representing quantitative style operations: color change, scale change, rotation, relocation, font weight, font style, letter spacing, and font family substitution. Text content editing (changing what text says) is not evaluated as a primary edit type; it is a well-studied dimension with distinct evaluation requirements. The style degradations measured here remain directly relevant to content editing, however, since unintended style changes are a common failure mode when models perform content edits. A judge's sensitivity to these degradations bounds its ability to detect content-editing side effects regardless of whether the nominal task is content or style.

\textit{Image domain.} Stimuli are browser-rendered HTML/CSS documents representing designed artifacts (posters, cards, infographics). This domain allows pixel-accurate ground truth and corresponds to the primary practical use case for quantitative style editing, which is overwhelmingly applied to designed media rather than naturalistic scenes. Generalization to scene text is not a goal.

\textit{Models evaluated.} Three proprietary and open-weight VLMs are evaluated as judges. The findings are specific to these model instances and should be interpreted as indicative of current frontier capabilities rather than exhaustive characterizations of any model class.

\textit{Evaluation scope.} The study characterises VLM sensitivity to rendered-text degradations with known parameters. Real editing models may fail in ways that look different from these controlled degradations, and that broader failure space is not covered here.
