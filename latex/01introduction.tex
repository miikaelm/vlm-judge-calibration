\chapter{Introduction} \label{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

\label{Background and Motivation}

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have rapidly advanced to the point where they can not only describe images but actively manipulate them through natural language instructions \cite{liu2023visualinstructiontuning, openai2024gpt4technicalreport}. This has generated considerable commercial interest: tools that previously required professional expertise in software like Adobe Photoshop can increasingly be operated by describing the desired change in words \cite{adobe2023firefly}. For this shift to be commercially viable, however, the models must not only produce plausible-looking outputs but execute edits with precision. A model that is asked to make a heading 20\% larger but also inadvertently changes its font weight or family has not done what was asked, even if the result looks superficially acceptable.

Text-in-image editing is one of the most commercially consequential domains for these models: posters, infographics, documents, marketing materials, and user interfaces all rely on text as a primary design element. Research on this domain has converged on a useful distinction between two kinds of edits. \textit{Content editing} changes what text says, replacing one word or phrase with another. This has received substantial attention and is increasingly reliable in both academic models \cite{tuo2024anytext, zeng2024textctrl} and commercial systems. \textit{Style editing} changes how text looks, such as its size, position, weight, color, or typeface, without altering its content. Quantitative style edits, where the target is numerically specified (``scale the title to 150\% of its current size'', ``move the caption 40 pixels to the right''), remain substantially harder. TextEditBench \cite{gui2025texteditbench} and WeEdit \cite{zhang2026weedit} have documented that current state-of-the-art models still fail frequently on these tasks, even as content editing has become routine.

The commercial stakes of this gap are concrete. When a generative editing model is deployed in a design tool, a style error that alters font weight alongside the requested color change, or shifts letter spacing when only a size adjustment was requested, is a real product failure. These are exactly the kinds of collateral damage that make users distrust and reject automated tools. Progress on quantitative style editing thus matters not just as a benchmark metric but as a prerequisite for models to function reliably in professional workflows.

\section{The Evaluation Problem}

\label{The Evaluation Problem}

To measure progress on text-in-image style editing, recent benchmarks have adopted an evaluation paradigm that is practical at scale: a Vision-Language Model (VLM) is presented with the original image, the edit instruction, and the model's output, and asked to score the quality of the edit on a rubric covering dimensions such as instruction following, visual consistency, and layout preservation. % [FIND REF: REDEdit-Bench and other frameworks using VLM judges]
This VLM-as-judge protocol is used in TextEditBench \cite{gui2025texteditbench}, WeEdit \cite{zhang2026weedit}, and related evaluation frameworks. The appeal is clear: human annotation at scale is expensive and slow, while VLMs can evaluate many examples quickly and at low cost.

The underlying assumption, that VLMs can reliably detect the kinds of errors text-editing models actually produce, has been treated as a given rather than a verified property. This is a significant gap. In the NLP domain, it has been extensively documented that LLM judges exhibit systematic biases: sensitivity to prompt framing, positional preferences, self-preference effects, and a tendency to give high scores regardless of quality. % [FIND REF: Zheng et al. 2023 MT-Bench, additional LLM-as-judge calibration papers]
Analogous questions about VLM judges in the visual domain have received far less scrutiny.

For text-in-image style editing specifically, the question is particularly pointed. Many of the edit dimensions that benchmarks ask VLMs to evaluate, such as font weight changes, small color shifts, or subtle positional displacements, are exactly the kinds of fine-grained typographic and spatial properties that VLMs are not known to be strong at. A VLM that cannot detect a 10\% scale error or a font family substitution is not a reliable judge of whether an editing model handled these operations correctly. If the judges are blind to certain error types or only sensitive at error magnitudes far above what constitutes a perceptible failure, then benchmark scores built on those judges provide a false sense of progress.

\section{This Work}

\label{This Work}

This thesis measures the reliability of VLM judges for text-in-image style editing directly. Rather than proposing a new evaluation protocol, we audit the existing one. We construct stimuli where the deviation from a correct edit is known with machine precision, feed them through the same VLM-as-judge pipeline that the field already uses, and observe how judge scores change as a function of error type and magnitude.

The stimulus design uses a browser rendering pipeline (HTML/CSS rendered via headless Chromium) to produce image triples: a source image, a correctly edited ground truth, and a degraded version with a controlled error applied on top of the correct edit. The degradation covers thirteen dimensions spanning typographic properties (font size, font weight, font family, font style, letter spacing, color), spatial properties (position, rotation, opacity), and image-level degradations (blur, noise, JPEG compression). Each dimension is sampled across multiple magnitude tiers, from sub-perceptual to clearly visible, to characterise the full sensitivity curve of each judge.

Three VLMs are evaluated as judges: GPT-4o, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, and Qwen2.5-VL. Each stimulus is evaluated under two experimental conditions. In Experiment 1 (perceptual sensitivity), the judge is shown the ground truth and degraded image and asked whether they differ. In Experiment 2 (edit judgment), the judge is shown the source image, the edit instruction, and the degraded result, replicating the exact benchmark task. The gap between the two experiments measures how much reasoning overhead costs the judge beyond raw visual discrimination.

\section{Research Questions}

\label{Research Questions}

\noindent \textbf{RQ1:} How reliably do VLM judges detect errors in text-in-image editing results, as measured by score sensitivity to controlled degradations?

\noindent \textbf{RQ1a:} Does judge reliability vary systematically across error types? Which degradation dimensions constitute blind spots for current VLM judges?

\noindent \textbf{RQ1b:} At what error magnitudes do VLM judges begin to reliably penalise degradations, and how do these thresholds compare to perceptual detectability?

\section{Scope and Boundaries}

\label{Scope and Boundaries}

\textit{Edit types.} This study covers eight edit types representing quantitative style operations: color change, scale change, rotation, relocation, font weight, font style, letter spacing, and font family substitution. Text content editing (changing what text says) is explicitly excluded; it is a well-studied dimension with distinct evaluation requirements.

\textit{Image domain.} Stimuli are browser-rendered HTML/CSS documents representing designed artifacts (posters, cards, infographics). This domain corresponds to the primary practical use case for quantitative style editing and allows pixel-accurate ground truth. Generalization to naturalistic scene text is not a goal.

\textit{Models evaluated.} Three proprietary and open-weight VLMs are evaluated as judges. The findings are specific to these model instances and should be interpreted as indicative of current frontier capabilities rather than exhaustive characterizations of any model class.

\textit{Evaluation scope.} The study characterises VLM sensitivity to rendered-text degradations with known parameters. It does not characterise VLM sensitivity to the full distribution of generative model failure modes, which may differ in visual character from rendered degradations. This scope boundary is discussed further in Section~\ref{Design Rationale}.
