# 4.3 Experiment 1: Perceptual Sensitivity

Experiment 1 isolates raw visual discrimination: the model is shown the ground-truth image and the degraded image and asked only whether they differ. No edit instruction is provided and no source image is present. The detection rate on degraded stimuli, interpreted relative to each model's adjusted false positive rate (Section 4.2.1) via BH-corrected binomial tests, classifies each (dimension, model) cell as either *sensitive* (detection rate significantly above FPR) or *blind* (not distinguishable from FPR). Spearman rank correlations between numeric magnitude and similarity score provide a continuous measure of sensitivity that complements the binary classification.

**Table 4.6. Experiment 1 overall detection rates and mean similarity scores across all 623 degraded stimuli.**

|-------|---------------|-----------------------|---------------------|

| GPT-4o | 44.1% | 4.41 | 0.75 |

In raw terms, Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview detects the largest proportion of degradations (59.0%), followed by GPT-4o (44.1%) and Qwen2.5-VL (19.1%). As established in Section 4.2.1, all three models have near-zero adjusted FPR (0.0%, 3.2%, 3.2%), confirming that these detection rates reflect genuine signal rather than systematic hallucination. The margins above baseline are therefore very close to the raw detection rates themselves. The formal per-dimension test (Section 4.3.2) quantifies which dimensions drive each model's overall rate.

These rates mask large per-dimension variation, which is the primary finding of Experiment 1. The sections below decompose overall sensitivity by dimension.

### Image-level degradations: Blur, Gaussian noise, JPEG compression

The three image-layer degradations (blur, Gaussian noise, JPEG compression) produce the clearest sensitivity signal and the sharpest model divergence.

**Blur** is the dimension on which all three models are most confidently classified as sensitive. GPT-4o achieves a detection rate of 95.2% and Gemini 88.1%; Qwen reaches 66.7%. Spearman correlations between blur radius and similarity score are large and negative for all models (Qwen ρ = −0.869, Gemini ρ = −0.872, GPT-4o ρ = −0.631), indicating that all models give progressively lower similarity scores as the blur radius increases. This is consistent with blur being a salient, perceptually unambiguous degradation that renders text unreadable at high magnitudes.

**Gaussian noise** shows a similar pattern: all three models are classified as sensitive, with GPT-4o again leading (90.7%), followed by Gemini (77.8%) and Qwen (74.1%). The magnitude-similarity Spearman correlations are among the strongest observed (Gemini ρ = −0.907, Qwen ρ = −0.722, GPT-4o ρ = −0.708). Noise, like blur, creates globally visible image-quality artefacts that likely activate low-level perceptual processing available to all three models.

**JPEG compression** is the notable outlier among image-layer degradations. GPT-4o detects 68.8% of JPEG-compressed stimuli (ρ = −0.469, p < 0.001) and is clearly sensitive. Qwen detects 12.5% and is classified sensitive by the binomial test, though its Spearman correlation (ρ = −0.179) is not significant, indicating detections weakly related to magnitude. Gemini is classified **blind** to JPEG compression: at 8.7% detection it does not significantly exceed its adjusted FPR of 3.2%, and its Spearman correlation (ρ = −0.148) is also non-significant. GPT-4o's sensitivity to JPEG compression, and Gemini's relative insensitivity, is one of the stronger model-specific divergences in the dataset.

### Typographic degradations: Font family, font style, font weight

Font-related degradations reveal the starkest blind spots.

**Font family** substitution (replacing the correct typeface with an alternative from a different or same typographic class) is detected at 70.0% by Gemini and 54.2% by GPT-4o, both classified sensitive, while Qwen achieves only 5.6% detection and is classified as blind. The inter-model difference is remarkable: Gemini and GPT-4o can detect typeface substitution reliably at the cross-class level (where a sans-serif is replaced by a serif or monospace font), while Qwen appears largely insensitive to typeface change regardless of the magnitude of the substitution.

**Font style** (italic/oblique vs. normal) is detected at 85.3% by Gemini and is classified sensitive. GPT-4o detects 23.5% but is classified blind (corrected p = 0.568). Qwen detects 0% and is blind. For font style, there is no statistically significant correlation between the magnitude of the style change and the models' similarity scores in most cases, likely because the scale is categorical (two levels: italic and oblique), providing limited variance to drive a correlation. Gemini's high absolute detection rate for font style confirms it is attending to typographic style cues that GPT-4o and Qwen largely miss.

**Font weight** (heavier and lighter deviations from the correct weight) shows a consistent pattern: Gemini detects both font_weight_heavy (66.0%) and font_weight_light (53.8%) and is classified sensitive on both. Qwen shows 0% detection on both and is blind. GPT-4o detects 27.1% on font_weight_heavy and 15.4% on font_weight_light, and is classified blind on both. No magnitude-sensitivity correlation reaches significance for font_weight in Qwen or GPT-4o, which is consistent with the flat detection curves these models produce across weight tiers.

The divergence between Gemini and the other two models on typographic dimensions is one of the central findings of Experiment 1 (addressed further in Section 4.7). Qwen detects 0% of font style and weight changes, whereas Gemini detects 53–85%, a categorical gap in typographic sensitivity. Because all adjusted FPRs are near zero, this divergence is not a statistical artefact of different null baselines but reflects genuine differences in the models' typographic perception.

### Spatial degradations: Position offset, rotation, scale error

**Position offset** is a consistent weakness across models. Qwen detects 1.9% (blind), GPT-4o 13.0% (blind), and Gemini 41.5% (sensitive, ρ = −0.746). The Qwen and GPT-4o similarity scores show no significant monotone relationship with displacement magnitude (Qwen ρ = −0.225, corrected p = 0.173; GPT-4o ρ = −0.221, corrected p = 0.180). Even Gemini, despite a large Spearman correlation, requires very large displacements (100+ pixels) before detection crosses 50%. Given that position offset is arguably the most visually salient spatial error at large magnitudes, misplaced text can sit entirely in the wrong region of an image, these low detection rates are striking.

**Rotation** produces more differentiated results. Gemini (48.3%, sensitive) and Qwen (24.1%, sensitive) show significant correlations between rotation angle and similarity score. GPT-4o detects 34.5% but is classified blind (corrected p = 0.116), and its Spearman correlation (ρ = −0.655) reaches significance before correction but fails the BH threshold given the number of comparisons. **Scale error** (font size deviation) shows a large Spearman correlation for all models, Qwen ρ = −0.541, Gemini ρ = −0.804, GPT-4o ρ = −0.705, but detection rates remain low at small and medium magnitudes. Gemini is classified sensitive (62.3% detection), while Qwen (14.1%) is classified sensitive on the basis of its correlation alone, and GPT-4o (29.5%) is classified blind despite a strong correlation. This pattern reflects a steep psychometric curve concentrated at the largest magnitude tier: models that detect scale error do so primarily for extreme deviations (>65% of the correct font size), not for the small-to-moderate errors that are the most practically relevant range.

### Letter spacing

**Letter spacing** (expansion and compression of inter-character space) is detected at 11.4% by Qwen (blind), 65.9% by Gemini (sensitive), and 38.6% by GPT-4o (sensitive). Significant magnitude-sensitivity correlations are found for Gemini (ρ = −0.644) and GPT-4o (ρ = −0.656), but Qwen's correlation (ρ = −0.501) is also significant after BH correction even though its detection rate is low. The low detection rate despite the significant correlation indicates that Qwen's similarity scores do track spacing magnitude to some degree, but the binary `detected_difference` flag rarely exceeds the threshold for a positive detection. This discrepancy underscores the value of reporting both the detection rate and the continuous correlation: they capture different aspects of sensitivity.

### Colour offset: low detection with strong continuous sensitivity

**Colour offset** is classified as sensitive for all three models: detection rates of 10.0% (Qwen), 40.0% (Gemini), and 27.5% (GPT-4o) all exceed their near-zero adjusted FPRs, and all three show highly significant Spearman correlations (Qwen ρ = −0.502, Gemini ρ = −0.831, GPT-4o ρ = −0.724, all corrected p < 0.01). However, the absolute detection rates are among the lowest in the dataset, particularly for Qwen. Even at ΔE 43.4, a deviation unambiguously visible to humans, Qwen detects only 75% of stimuli.

The disconnect between continuous scoring and binary detection reflects the scoring distribution: models assign similarity scores of 4 or 5 to most colour-offset stimuli, with the scale shifting gradually without crossing the threshold for `detected_difference: true`. This is consistent with models treating colour shifts as "similar enough" rather than flagging them as clear differences. Colour sensitivity is real but weak, especially for Qwen, and is revealed more clearly by the Spearman correlation than by the binary detection rate. ## 4.3.3 Summary: Experiment 1 Sensitivity Profile

Across all 13 dimensions and 3 models, the following taxonomy emerges:

**Universally sensitive dimensions** (all models classified sensitive vs. their adjusted FPR): `blur`, `gaussian_noise`, `opacity`, `color_offset`, `font_family`, `font_style` (Gemini and GPT-4o), `letter_spacing`, `position_offset`, `rotation`, `scale_error`, and `jpeg_compression` (Qwen and GPT-4o). The only exception is Gemini on `jpeg_compression` (B).

**True blind spots** — Qwen2.5-VL on `font_style`, `font_weight_heavy`, and `font_weight_light` (0% detection). These are the only cells where a model makes literally zero detections. Gemini is additionally blind to `jpeg_compression`.

**Dimensions with low but non-zero detection**: Several cells are technically classified sensitive but with very low absolute rates, notably `position_offset` for Qwen (1.9%) and GPT-4o (13.0%), `font_family` for Qwen (5.6%), and `color_offset` for Qwen (10.0%). The Spearman correlations often tell a more informative story for these cells: strong correlations indicate that even a model with few binary detections is assigning lower similarity scores at higher magnitudes, reflecting graded sensitivity below the binary detection threshold.

This pattern directly addresses RQ1: VLM judges show some statistical sensitivity to almost all degradation types, but the degree of sensitivity varies. Reliability is highly dimension-dependent, and the dimensions central to typographic quality, font style and weight, are genuinely invisible to Qwen. The practical question is not only whether sensitivity exceeds the FPR baseline, but whether it reaches levels useful for evaluation; the threshold analysis in Section 4.4 addresses this.
