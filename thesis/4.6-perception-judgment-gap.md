# 4.6 The Perception-to-Judgment Gap

The two-experiment design allows a direct comparison between Experiment 1 (perceptual sensitivity: can the model see the error?) and Experiment 2 (edit judgment: does the model penalise the error?). For each matched stimulus, the score gap is defined as:

$$\text{Gap}(d, m) = \overline{\text{NM}_{E2}}_{d,m} - \overline{\text{sim}_{E1}}_{d,m}$$

where $\text{NM}_{E2}$ is the Experiment 2 `narrow_mean` (mean of `instruction_following`, `text_accuracy`, `visual_consistency`, `layout_preservation`) and $\text{sim}_{E1}$ is the Experiment 1 similarity score. `narrow_mean` is used as the primary gap metric because it is cross-model comparable: the noop control shows all four narrow dimensions are scored consistently near 1 across models when no edit is applied, whereas `overall_quality` diverges by 2.62 points (Section 4.2.3, Section 4.5.5). A gap computed from `overall_quality` levels would conflate genuine sensitivity differences with cross-model baseline offsets.

A negative gap indicates that Experiment 2 `narrow_mean` is lower than Experiment 1 similarity score on the same stimuli — the model penalises the edit more harshly under the judgment task than its perceptual score would predict. A positive gap indicates the opposite. The cross-experiment Spearman correlation between similarity (Exp1) and `overall_quality` (Exp2) on matched stimuli quantifies global coherence between the two assessment modes. This correlation uses raw `overall_quality` deliberately: it is a within-model rank analysis, not a cross-model level comparison, so the scale offset does not affect interpretation.

All ρ(sim↔OQ) correlations are positive and significant (p < 0.001), confirming that models which give higher similarity scores in Experiment 1 also tend to give higher `overall_quality` scores in Experiment 2. The strength of this relationship differs substantially across models.

**Gemini** shows a strong global correlation (ρ(sim↔OQ) = 0.738, ρ(sim↔NM) = 0.755). Both metrics agree: Gemini's perceptual judgment and its edit quality judgment are tightly coupled. Using `narrow_mean` as the Exp2 metric very slightly strengthens the correlation, consistent with the narrow dimensions capturing the same perceptual signal as OQ without the holistic scope noise.

**GPT-4o** shows a moderate global correlation (ρ(sim↔OQ) = 0.492, ρ(sim↔NM) = 0.509). Its perceptual and judgment scores are related but not tightly coupled. The near-identical values for OQ and NM confirm that GPT-4o's OQ holistic judgment tracks the narrow dims closely.

**Qwen2.5-VL** shows the weakest global correlation (ρ(sim↔OQ) = 0.301, ρ(sim↔NM) = 0.224). The `narrow_mean` correlation is weaker than OQ for Qwen — an important result. The reason is that `narrow_mean` averages in `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation`, which are near-constant at ceiling for Qwen, adding noise that attenuates the correlation. Qwen's OQ, despite its scale-offset problem, captures more perceptual variation than its narrow_mean does, because OQ integrates the holistic signal that `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` are missing. Regardless of which metric is used, Qwen's cross-experiment coherence is the lowest: neither OQ nor narrow_mean closely tracks Exp1 perceptual judgments, confirming that Qwen's Experiment 2 scores are driven largely by factors independent of visual perception.

### The NM gap story differs substantially from the OQ gap story

The most important observation from Table 4.13 is that the `narrow_mean` gap and the OQ gap tell different stories for most dimensions. The structural reason is the same one that makes `narrow_mean` more cross-model comparable: `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` stay near ceiling even when OQ collapses, which pushes NM gaps toward zero or positive values relative to OQ gaps. This means the NM gap measures whether `instruction_following` and `visual_consistency` — the discriminatory narrow dims — fall below or above Exp1 similarity, without the floor-collapsing effect of OQ's holistic judgment.

### Gemini: OQ amplification does not carry over to narrow_mean

Gemini's OQ score gaps are the most negative in the table — Gaussian noise (−1.96), blur (−1.05), font_style (−1.24), font_family (−0.88) — and its per-dimension correlations are consistently significant. However, in `narrow_mean`, Gemini's gaps are positive for almost every dimension: blur +0.34, gaussian_noise −0.24, font_family +0.24, font_style −0.07, font_weight_heavy +0.26, font_weight_light +0.14, letter_spacing +0.49, opacity +0.07, position_offset +0.02, rotation +0.29, scale_error +0.47. Only gaussian_noise (−0.24), jpeg_compression (−0.23), and font_style (−0.07) have negative NM gaps.

This means Gemini's `narrow_mean` is at or above Exp1 similarity for most dimensions — the instruction-reasoning step does not penalise more harshly than Exp1 would predict, when measured through the four narrow dimensions. The severe OQ penalties Gemini applies (blur OQ 1.66, noise OQ 1.57) are a holistic-judgment effect: Gemini's "usability" assessment collapses for heavily degraded images, but `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` remain high, pulling NM up. The ρ(sim↔OQ) correlations remain the best available per-dimension coherence measure, since they use OQ's fuller dynamic range.

### Qwen: reasoning-without-perception visible in narrow_mean

Qwen's NM gaps show the reasoning-without-perception pattern more clearly than any other model. For dimensions where Qwen is blind in Experiment 1 — giving similarity scores near 5.0 — it assigns NM scores of approximately 3.94–4.24 in Experiment 2, producing negative NM gaps:

- **font_weight_light**: NM gap −1.06, ρ = n/s. Exp1 sim = 5.0 (zero variance, completely blind). Exp2 NM = 3.94.
- **font_weight_heavy**: NM gap −0.82, ρ = n/s. Exp1 sim ≈ 5.0. Exp2 NM = 4.18.
- **font_style**: NM gap −0.78, ρ = n/s. Exp1 sim ≈ 5.0. Exp2 NM = 4.22.
- **font_family**: NM gap −0.77, ρ = n/s. Exp1 sim ≈ 4.94. Exp2 NM = 4.17.
- **position_offset**: NM gap −0.89, ρ = n/s. Exp1 sim ≈ 4.98. Exp2 NM = 4.09.

The per-dimension Spearman correlations are non-significant for all of these (ρ = n/s), confirming that the NM variation is not grounded in perceptual signal. Qwen's Experiment 2 scores for these dimensions are responding to cues in the edit instruction (mentioning "position", "weight", "font family") even though the model demonstrably cannot see the relevant error in Experiment 1.

In contrast, for image-level degradations (blur, gaussian_noise), Qwen shows positive NM gaps (+0.12 and +0.22), with significant negative magnitude correlations. Here, Qwen's narrow_mean does track the degradation magnitude, and its Exp2 aggregate score is somewhat above Exp1 similarity — consistent with Qwen applying the edit instruction reasoning to confirm that blur and noise are visible failures.

### GPT-4o: JPEG compression inflation preserved in narrow_mean

GPT-4o's positive JPEG compression gap survives the transition to `narrow_mean`: NM gap = +0.64, OQ gap = +0.54. Both metrics give NM/OQ above Exp1 similarity (4.95 NM, 4.85 OQ vs Exp1 sim 4.31). This confirms that the suppression of GPT-4o's compression response is distributed across the narrow rubric dimensions, not concentrated only in the holistic OQ. When GPT-4o evaluates JPEG-compressed edit results, it rates both the narrow dimensions and the overall quality above what its perceptual comparison would predict — the edit judgment context actively suppresses its response to compression artefacts.

### `color_offset` gap: positive for all models in narrow_mean

All three models show positive NM gaps for colour offset (Qwen +0.15, Gemini +0.30, GPT-4o +0.11), meaning Experiment 2 `narrow_mean` exceeds Experiment 1 similarity for colour-change stimuli. For Qwen this is a ceiling artefact (NM = 5.00, both experiments near ceiling). For Gemini, the positive NM gap (+0.30 vs OQ gap +0.05) reflects that instruction-level reasoning does not help detect colour errors — the NM stays high even though Gemini does show significant magnitude sensitivity for colour offset in NM (ρ = −0.563***). For GPT-4o, the NM gap is small (+0.11) and colour offset NM shows significant magnitude sensitivity (ρ = −0.445**).

## 4.6.3 Transition from Unreliable to Reliable Judgment: Magnitude Dependence

For dimensions where Experiment 2 OQ tracks magnitude (significant Spearman ρ, Table 4.13), the transition from unreliable to reliable judgment in Experiment 2 occurs at tier levels that are generally consistent with or later than the Experiment 1 thresholds established in Section 4.4. For example:

- Gemini's blur OQ mean: small 2.69 → medium 1.29 → large 1.07. The transition is abrupt between small and medium. Experiment 1 threshold was small tier (radius ≈1.3), and Experiment 2 OQ reaches near-floor also at small/medium.
- Qwen's Gaussian noise OQ: small 3.89 → medium 2.67 → large 1.94. Transition is gradual and reaches low-but-not-floor values at large.

For dimensions where Experiment 2 OQ does not track magnitude (non-significant ρ, Table 4.13), there is no transition — the model assigns a roughly constant quality score regardless of how severe the degradation is. These flat profiles confirm that the judgment task does not help models detect errors they miss in the perceptual task.

## 4.6.4 Summary: Gap Analysis Implications for RQ1b

The score gap analysis answers RQ1b at the experiment-level: the instruction-reasoning step in Experiment 2 does not systematically improve the reliability of detection compared to Experiment 1. Using `narrow_mean` as the primary metric:

- For **Gemini**, NM gaps are near-zero or positive on most dimensions. The instruction-reasoning step does not amplify penalties beyond what Exp1 similarity would predict in aggregate — the harsh OQ penalties Gemini applies are a holistic-judgment effect not shared by the narrow dims.
- For **Qwen**, NM gaps are negative on dimensions Qwen cannot perceive (font weight, font style, position), confirming reasoning-without-perception: penalisation not grounded in visual evidence.
- For **GPT-4o**, the positive JPEG compression NM gap (+0.64) is the clearest anomaly: instruction context actively suppresses GPT-4o's response to compression artefacts across all narrow dimensions.

The OQ gaps (within-model reference) tell a more dramatic story for Gemini (−1.05 to −1.96 on image degradations) because OQ's holistic scope captures image-quality failures that the narrow dimensions partly miss. Both metrics are reported in Table 4.13 and each is useful in its respective context.

The central implication is that the benchmark-facing task (Experiment 2) provides no additional sensitivity over the perceptual task (Experiment 1) for the dimensions that matter most — spatial position, scale, colour, and font properties — and introduces its own artefacts. A model that cannot see a colour error in Experiment 1 will not catch it in Experiment 2 either, and may assign high quality scores to colour-error stimuli while appearing to "follow" the instruction.
