## 4.7.2 Blind Spot Summary

With all models having near-zero adjusted FPR (0.0–3.2%), the blind/sensitive classification is close to testing against chance. The remaining blind spots are therefore genuinely flat dimensions: **Qwen2.5-VL on `font_style`, `font_weight_heavy`, and `font_weight_light`** (0% detection) and **Gemini on `jpeg_compression`** (8.7% detection barely exceeds its 3.2% FPR but not at significance after BH correction). No other (dimension, model) cell is classified blind.

There are no universally blind dimensions: every dimension is sensitive for at least two of the three models. Colour offset, which would have been universally blind under the models' raw (unadjusted) FPRs, is sensitive for all three once the `alignment_error` artefact is removed from the null baseline — an illustration of how the choice of FPR baseline meaningfully affects which dimensions appear problematic.

## 4.7.3 Model-Specific Sensitivity Patterns

**Qwen2.5-VL** is the least sensitive model overall. It makes zero detections on `font_style`, `font_weight_heavy`, and `font_weight_light` — a categorical absence of sensitivity to typographic style and weight changes. Its detection rates for `font_family` (5.6%), `position_offset` (1.9%), and `color_offset` (10.0%) are classified sensitive against the near-zero FPR but are very low in absolute terms. Qwen's clearest sensitivity is concentrated on image-level degradations (`blur` 66.7%, `gaussian_noise` 74.1%, `opacity` 24.1%) and gross spatial changes (`rotation` 24.1%).

**GPT-4o** is classified sensitive on all 13 dimensions, with detection rates spanning from 13.0% (`position_offset`) to 95.2% (`blur`). It shows strong sensitivity on image-level degradations and moderate sensitivity on spatial and typographic dimensions. There is no categorical zero-detection blind spot for GPT-4o, though `position_offset` (13.0%), `font_weight_light` (15.4%), and `font_style` (23.5%) reflect relatively weak practical sensitivity.

**Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview** shows the broadest and most consistent sensitivity, with model-specific blind spots limited to `jpeg_compression`. It leads on typographic dimensions (font family 70.0%, font style 85.3%, font weight heavy 66.0%, font weight light 53.8%, letter spacing 65.9%) and also shows strong sensitivity to image-level and spatial changes. Because its adjusted FPR is 3.2%, Gemini faces a slightly higher bar than Qwen (FPR 0.0%) for the binomial test, but its detection rates on sensitive dimensions comfortably exceed this baseline.

## 4.7.4 Inter-Model Agreement: Fleiss' Kappa

**Table 4.15. Fleiss' kappa for inter-model agreement on blind/sensitive classifications across 13 dimensions.**

| | Fleiss' κ |
|--|---|
| Across all 13 dimensions (3 models) | **−0.114** |

Fleiss' kappa of −0.114 is negative, falling below the chance-agreement baseline. This reflects the mathematical behaviour of kappa under strong base-rate imbalance: with near-zero adjusted FPR for all models, almost all dimensions are classified sensitive, leaving very few B cells (Qwen on font_style and font_weights, Gemini on jpeg_compression). When one category dominates, any occasional disagreement produces a negative kappa even if the agreement pattern is systematic.

The kappa should be read alongside the raw detection rates rather than as a stand-alone agreement metric. The models do diverge meaningfully in *degree* of sensitivity on many dimensions — the Spearman correlations and tier-level detection curves (Section 4.4) reveal this variation more clearly than the binary label.

## 4.7.5 Sensitivity Ranking

Ordering degradation dimensions by mean detection rate across models (Table 4.7 / outputs/figures/psychometric/detection_rate_dim_by_model.png) yields:

**Most sensitive (mean DR > 70%):** `blur` (83%), `gaussian_noise` (80%)

**Moderately sensitive (mean DR 35–70%):** `font_family` (43%), `font_style` (36%), `opacity` (39%), `letter_spacing` (39%), `font_weight_heavy` (31%), `rotation` (36%), `scale_error` (35%)

**Poorly detected (mean DR < 30%):** `jpeg_compression` (29%), `font_weight_light` (23%), `color_offset` (26%), `position_offset` (18%)

The ordering reveals that image-level degradations top the sensitivity ranking, while colour and spatial position anchor the bottom. This ordering is consistent with the hypothesis that VLMs have been trained on natural-image data where global image quality (noise, blur) is a salient and common dimension, while fine-grained typographic properties and precise spatial positioning are less consistently labelled or attended to during training.

## 4.7.6 Summary: RQ1a

RQ1a asked how detection sensitivity varies across error types. The answer has three components:

1. **Variation is large.** Across dimensions, detection rates range from 0% (Qwen on font style and font weight) to 95% (GPT-4o on blur). This is not a small calibration difference but a categorical divergence between reliably detected and barely detected error types.

2. **The variation follows a consistent pattern.** Image-level quality degradations (blur, noise) produce the highest detection rates across all models. Typographic fine-structure (font weight, font style) and spatial positioning are weakly detected or undetected, particularly for Qwen. The pattern aligns with the likely distribution of visual properties in VLM training data: global image quality is a universal signal, fine-grained typographic properties and spatial coordinates are not.

3. **The models disagree on which dimensions fall in between.** For dimensions in the middle sensitivity range, models diverge substantially in detection rates even when all are technically classified sensitive. The near-zero adjusted FPRs mean the sensitive/blind binary is nearly always positive; the meaningful variation lies in detection rates and Spearman correlations. This graded divergence means the choice of judge model determines not only which errors are caught but how reliably each error type is flagged.
