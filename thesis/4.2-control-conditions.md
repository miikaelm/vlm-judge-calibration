# 4.2 Control Condition Results

The three control conditions defined in Section 3.4.5 establish the baselines against which all main results are interpreted: the identity control verifies that models are not systematically biased toward false detections, the source-target control establishes whether models can detect correctly executed edits at all, and the instruction-not-followed control tests whether the Experiment 2 rubric is measuring instruction adherence.

## 4.2.1 Identity Control: False Positive Rates (Experiment 1)

In the identity control, the model is shown an identical image pair — the correctly edited image presented as both the reference and the comparison — and should report `detected_difference: false` and `similarity_score: 5`. Any deviation is a false positive: the model is hallucinating a difference that does not exist. Each control set contains 46 stimuli spanning the edit-type distribution of the main corpus.

Examining the identity control by degradation dimension reveals that false positive rates are heavily concentrated in a single dimension. Table 4.3 shows the full per-dimension breakdown.

**Table 4.3. Identity control: false positive rates by degradation dimension and model (Experiment 1). Dimensions marked † are excluded from the adjusted model-level FPR used as the binomial-test baseline (see below).**

| Degradation dimension | Qwen2.5-VL | Gemini 3.1 Pro | GPT-4o |
|---|---|---|---|
| `alignment_error` † | 13.3% | 73.3% | 60.0% |
| `color_offset` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| `font_family` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| `font_style` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| `font_weight` | 0.0% | 20.0% | 20.0% |
| `letter_spacing` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| `rotation` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |
| `scale_error` | 0.0% | 0.0% | 0.0% |

The `alignment_error` dimension is the sole source of substantial false positive errors. All other dimensions yield near-zero FPR across all three models, with the only non-zero entry being `font_weight` at 20.0% for Gemini and GPT-4o. Excluding `alignment_error`, the model-level FPR collapses to 0.0% (Qwen), 3.2% (Gemini), and 3.2% (GPT-4o), compared to raw values of 4.3%, 26.1%, and 21.7% when `alignment_error` is included. The high `alignment_error` FPR likely arises because models appear to fire their spatial-anomaly detectors in response to the relocated text's unusual position relative to their internal layout priors, rather than genuinely detecting a difference between the two identical images. This is likely a hallucinatory response to the image content, not a comparison error.

**Table 4.4. Identity control: false positive rates and mean similarity scores (Experiment 1). "FPR (adjusted)" excludes `alignment_error`; "FPR (all dims)" includes all dimensions.**

| Model | n | FPR (adjusted) | FPR (all dims) | Mean similarity score |
|-------|---|----------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| Qwen2.5-VL | 46 | **0.0%** | 4.3% | 4.96 |
| Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview | 46 | 3.2% | 26.1% | 4.52 |
| GPT-4o | 46 | 3.2% | 21.7% | 4.72 |

The adjusted FPR values (0.0%, 3.2%, 3.2%) confirm that, outside of the `alignment_error` spatial hallucination, all three models are effectively unbiased: they do not systematically report differences in identical image pairs. All three models assign mean similarity scores close to 5 on identical pairs (4.96, 4.52, 4.72), further supporting this interpretation.

These adjusted FPR values serve as each model's individual null baseline for the blind/sensitive classification in Section 4.3. A dimension is considered a genuine blind spot only when the model's detection rate on degraded stimuli does not significantly exceed its adjusted FPR (one-sided binomial test with BH correction). Because all adjusted FPRs are near zero (0–3.2%), even modest detection rates can pass the test for all three models.

## 4.2.2 Source-Target Control: Sensitivity Floor (Experiment 1)

The source-target control presents the model with the correctly executed edit as the reference and the original unedited source image as the comparison. Because a correctly applied edit *does* look different from the source, reporting `detected_difference: true` is the correct response. Low detection on these stimuli establishes the sensitivity floor: if a model cannot detect a correctly executed edit when confronted directly with the difference between source and ground truth, failure to detect degraded stimuli cannot be attributed to degradation invisibility alone, the model may be insensitive to the relevant visual change regardless of whether a degradation error is introduced.

**Table 4.4. Source-target control: detection rate of the ground-truth edit vs. source and mean similarity scores (Experiment 1).**

| Model | n | Detection rate | Mean similarity score |
|-------|---|----------------|----------------------|
| Qwen2.5-VL | 34 | 14.7% | 4.74 |
| Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview | 32 | **93.8%** | 3.69 |
| GPT-4o | 34 | 55.9% | 4.27 |

Model behaviour on the source-target control diverges sharply. Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview detects 93.8% of ground-truth edits and assigns a mean similarity score of 3.69, close to the midpoint of the scale, reflecting genuine sensitivity to the visual difference between source and correctly-edited image. GPT-4o detects 55.9% and gives a mean similarity score of 4.27, showing moderate sensitivity. Qwen2.5-VL detects only 14.7% of ground-truth edits and gives a mean similarity score of 4.74: for Qwen, the source and the correctly-executed edit look nearly identical, which is consistent with its broader pattern of very low detection rates across most dimensions in the main experiment.

The source-target detection rates establish that the models are ordered Gemini > GPT-4o > Qwen in their sensitivity to actual visual changes. Crucially, Qwen's low detection rate here confirms that its near-zero detection rates on several degradation dimensions (Section 4.3) reflect genuine visual insensitivity to those edit types, not merely task uncertainty.

## 4.2.3 Instruction-Not-Followed Control: Rubric Validity (Experiment 2)

The instruction-not-followed control presents the model with the original unedited image as the "edit result" alongside the full edit instruction, so no edit was applied. Per-dimension expectations are not uniform. instruction_following should be 1, since the requested operation was not performed. text_accuracy, visual_consistency, and layout_preservation should be high: the unedited image contains correctly rendered text, visually intact rendering, and an unchanged layout. In the noop condition, the image is unmodified, so the properties these dimensions describe, text correctness, visual integrity, and layout, are intact by construction. The behaviour of overall_quality is the open question, since the prompt does not define what scope the dimension should encompass, and the label admits multiple plausible readings.

Table 4.5. Instruction-not-followed control: mean Experiment 2 scores when no edit was applied (n = 33–34 per model).

| Dimension              | Qwen2.5-VL | Gemini 3.1 Pro | GPT-4o |
|------------------------|------------|----------------|--------|
| instruction_following  | 1.71       | 1.00           | 1.71   |
| text_accuracy          | 4.65       | 5.00           | 5.00   |
| visual_consistency     | 3.71       | 5.00           | 5.00   |
| layout_preservation    | 5.00       | 5.00           | 5.00   |
| overall_quality        | 3.62       | 1.00           | 2.94   |

The four narrow dimensions behave largely as expected. All three models score layout_preservation at the ceiling, and Gemini and GPT-4o score text_accuracy and visual_consistency at the ceiling as well. Qwen's slightly lower scores on text_accuracy (4.65) and visual_consistency (3.71) suggest some sensitivity to image properties unrelated to the edit, but the values remain high. instruction_following is correctly identified as failed by all three models, with Gemini at the floor (1.00) and Qwen and GPT-4o close to it (1.71).

The divergence is concentrated in overall_quality. Gemini assigns a mean of 1.00, with every noop stimulus receiving the minimum score uniformly across edit types. GPT-4o assigns a mean of 2.94, with 67.7% of noop stimuli scoring 3 or higher. Qwen assigns a mean of 3.62, with 73.5% of stimuli scoring 3 or higher. The same stimuli, evaluated against the same prompt, produce scores spanning 2.62 points on a five-point scale depending on which model is judging.

The pattern across dimensions localises the source of the divergence. The four narrow dimensions, which name what they measure with enough specificity to leave little room for interpretation, produce broad agreement. The one dimension whose label does not specify scope produces broad disagreement. The most economical reading is that overall_quality is under-specified by the prompt, and that each model resolves the scope according to a different prior. The implications for downstream analysis and benchmark design are taken up in Section 4.5.5; but generally, two consequences follow. First, overall_quality is reported alongside, rather than in place of, the narrow dimensions throughout Sections 4.5 and 4.6, so that readers can see whether a finding holds where the rubric is well-defined. Second, the narrow-dimension mean (narrow_mean) is used as the primary aggregate where a single Experiment 2 score is needed, since its noop spread across models is 0.41 points compared to 2.62 for overall_quality.
