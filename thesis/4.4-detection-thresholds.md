# 4.4 Detection Thresholds

This section addresses RQ1b: at what degradation magnitudes do VLM judges transition from unreliable to reliable detection? The threshold analysis uses the discrete JND estimate defined in Section 3.4.3: the smallest magnitude tier at which detection rate reaches or exceeds 50% (and separately 75%), expressed both as a tier label and as the mean numeric magnitude in physical units for that tier. For dimensions or models where no tier reaches the threshold, the entry is "not crossed." (NOTE: What was JND exactly again?)

Psychometric curves for each dimension are shown in outputs/figures/psychometric/ (one panel per dimension, with one line per model). The curves plot mean detection rate (Experiment 1) against magnitude tier with bootstrap 95% confidence intervals. The tier-level data reported here and summarised in Table 4.8 are derived from the same source.

## 4.4.2 Image-Level Degradations

### Blur

The psychometric curves for blur are the steepest and most consistent of any dimension. GPT-4o and Gemini both reach 50% detection at the small tier (mean blur radius ≈1.34), and GPT-4o maintains 93% detection even at the small tier, its curve is essentially at ceiling by the first tier sampled. Qwen requires medium blur (radius ≈3.22) to cross 50%, and its curve is approximately linear across the three tiers (small: 14.3%, medium: 85.7%, large: 100%). The 75% threshold for Qwen coincides with its 50% threshold (medium tier), because its curve jumps sharply between small and medium.

The implication is that GPT-4o and Gemini can reliably detect any blur that is perceptible to a human observer viewing two images side by side, their threshold is at or below the perceptual just-noticeable difference for blur. Qwen requires blur that is substantially visible even in isolation (radius > 3 pixels on a 512×512 image).

### Gaussian Noise

GPT-4o's 50% threshold is at the small noise tier (σ ≈ 4.7), reaching 72% detection at this level. Gemini and Qwen both cross 50% at the medium tier (σ ≈ 16.8), with Gemini reaching 94.4% and Qwen 94.4% at that tier. All three models reach 100% detection at the large tier (σ ≈ 31.8). The curves are steep: there is a substantial jump from small to medium for Gemini and Qwen, with near-ceiling performance thereafter.

### JPEG Compression

GPT-4o is the only model with a defined threshold for JPEG compression. Its 50% threshold falls at the medium tier (quality loss ≈ 49.7 units, corresponding to JPEG quality approximately 50 on a 0–100 scale). At the small tier (light compression, quality ≈ 80), GPT-4o detects only 37.5%; at the large tier (heavy compression, quality ≈ 24), detection reaches 87.5%. This is a relatively late threshold — quality-50 JPEG produces visible block artefacts in regions of fine detail, but is far from the heaviest compression applied. Qwen's curve is flat across tiers (6.3%, 12.5%, 18.8%), and Gemini's curve is similarly flat (6.3%, 7.1%, 12.5%) — neither shows a meaningful monotone rise, consistent with their blind classifications.

## 4.4.3 Typographic Degradations

### Font Family

The font_family dimension uses a two-tier magnitude encoding: same-class substitutions (e.g., one sans-serif for another, magnitude 0.5) and cross-class substitutions (e.g., serif for sans-serif, magnitude 1.0). Gemini and GPT-4o both cross 50% at the large (cross-class) tier, with detection rates of 100% (Gemini) and 86.7% (GPT-4o) on cross-class substitutions. At the same-class (medium) tier, Gemini detects 47.5% and GPT-4o 31.0%. Qwen is at 0.0% and 13.3% respectively, with the large-tier rate barely above its FPR. The results indicate that cross-class font substitutions are reliably detectable by Gemini and GPT-4o, but same-class substitutions — more typographically subtle — are near-chance for all models. Qwen is effectively blind to typeface changes entirely.

### Font Style

Gemini's 50% threshold for font_style is at the medium tier (italic vs. normal, categorical magnitude 0.5), with a detection rate of 76.5%. Detection rises to 94.1% at the large tier (oblique). GPT-4o and Qwen do not cross 50% at any tier (Qwen: 0% at both; GPT-4o: 17.7% and 29.4%). The very high Gemini detection rate at the lower magnitude tier implies that Gemini is sensitive to italic rendering in a way that GPT-4o and Qwen are not.

### Font Weight

Gemini's 50% threshold for font_weight_heavy is at the small tier (weight +100 CSS units, e.g., changing from 400 to 500), with a detection rate of 52.4%. Detection at the xxlarge tier (+500 units, from 400 to 900) reaches 78.3%. The non-monotone pattern at intermediate tiers (medium: 100% at +200; xlarge: 0% at +400; xxlarge: 78.3% at +500) reflects very small per-tier sample sizes (n = 2 at medium, n = 1 at xlarge) and should not be over-interpreted. For font_weight_light (lighter direction), Gemini also crosses 50% at the small tier. GPT-4o and Qwen show no reliable detection at any weight tier.

## 4.4.4 Spatial Degradations

### Position Offset

The position offset dimension uses pixel displacement in three tiers: small (≈7 px), medium (≈31 px), and large (≈101 px). Gemini's detection rates are 5.6% → 29.4% → 88.9%, crossing 50% only at the large tier (>100 px displacement). Qwen's curve is 0.0%, 0.0%, 5.6% — essentially zero across all tiers. GPT-4o's curve is 5.6%, 16.7%, 16.7% — no monotone rise. Gemini's 50% threshold of 100+ pixels means that only displacements equivalent to roughly 20% of the image width produce reliable detection. Smaller displacements — including the 3–50 px range that would correspond to noticeable but moderate spatial errors in real editing outputs — are invisible to all three models.

### Rotation

Rotation thresholds converge across Gemini and GPT-4o at the large tier (~18°). Below this, at the medium tier (~6.5°), both models detect approximately 43% of stimuli — just below the 50% criterion. Qwen's curve is 0%, 0%, 14.3%, 25.0%, 71.4% across the five tiers (tiny through xlarge), crossing 50% only at the xlarge tier (~23°). These thresholds are considerably higher than the ~1–3° at which a careful human observer would notice a misalignment on a document element. All three models require gross rotations equivalent to a 15–25° tilt before reliable detection begins.

### Scale Error

Scale error threshold results are among the most practically significant in the study. Gemini's 50% threshold is at the large tier (25% size deviation), reaching 77.3% detection. GPT-4o and Qwen both reach 50% only at the xxlarge tier (77.4% size deviation) — a font that is 77% too large or too small. Detection rates below this level are near zero for GPT-4o (0%, 0%, 9.1%, 16.7% across small through xlarge) and Qwen (0%, 0%, 0%, 0%). Even Gemini shows only 14.3% and 16.7% detection at the small (3.5%) and medium (9.9%) tiers. Scale errors in the 5–25% range — which would be clearly visible to human annotators and represent meaningful execution errors — are effectively invisible to two of the three models and nearly invisible to the third.

## 4.4.5 Perceptual Degradations

### Opacity

All three models require medium-to-large opacity reductions for detection. At the small tier (≈5% opacity reduction), all three models detect 0.0%. Gemini crosses 50% at the medium tier (61.1% detection, ≈30% opacity reduction). Qwen and GPT-4o both cross 50% only at the large tier (55.6% and 88.9% respectively, ≈69% opacity reduction). A 30% reduction in opacity — where text appears noticeably faded — thus exceeds Gemini's threshold but not Qwen's or GPT-4o's; 70% opacity reduction, where text is quite transparent, is required for both Qwen and GPT-4o.

### Letter Spacing

Gemini crosses 50% at the medium letter-spacing tier (3.5 px expansion), with a detection rate of 70.6%. GPT-4o crosses 50% at the large tier (11.4 px), reaching 80.0%. Qwen does not cross 50% at any tier (0.0%, 5.9%, 40.0%). The large tier for letter spacing (8–15 px) represents a visually conspicuous expansion that changes the overall width of a text string substantially. Qwen's failure to reach 50% even at this tier, despite approaching it (40.0%), is consistent with its blind classification.

## 4.4.6 Summary: Threshold Implications for RQ1b

The threshold analysis directly answers RQ1b. The transition from unreliable to reliable detection occurs at very different magnitudes depending on the dimension and the model:

- For **image-level degradations** (blur, Gaussian noise), the transition occurs at magnitudes that are perceptible on direct comparison — especially for GPT-4o and Gemini. These dimensions are within the range of reliable human-level discrimination.

- For **typographic and spatial degradations**, the transition occurs at magnitudes far above what human observers would require. Scale errors of 5–25%, positional displacements of 10–50 px, and colour deviations below ΔE 30 are all effectively invisible to most models, yet these correspond to exactly the error range that real text-editing systems produce.

- For **colour**, the threshold is remarkably late (Qwen: ΔE 43.4; GPT-4o: ΔE 15.4; Gemini: ΔE 10.6 as the 50% tier), and even at the largest tier the threshold is not crossed in the Fisher's exact test for most models, consistent with the universal blind classification.

The practical implication is that for the majority of error types, current VLM judges operate at thresholds that are far above the error magnitudes likely encountered in real text-editing evaluation. A model that produces a font-size error of 15% or a colour deviation of ΔE 10 would not be reliably penalised by any of the three judges. This directly limits the validity of VLM-based evaluation in the benchmarks that rely on these models.
