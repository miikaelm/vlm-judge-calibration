# 4.5 Experiment 2: Edit Judgment Sensitivity

Experiment 2 replicates the actual benchmark task: the model is given the source image, the edit instruction, and the degraded result, and asked to score the result on the five-dimension rubric used by TextEditBench. No ground-truth reference is provided. The model must infer what a correct result would look like from the instruction alone and compare it against the degraded output. This section reports rubric score descriptives, internal consistency, per-dimension magnitude sensitivity, and a focused analysis of the `overall_quality` dimension for each model.

## 4.5.1 Cross-Model Comparability and the Choice of Aggregate

Cross-model comparisons and model rankings throughout this section use `narrow_mean`, the mean of the four narrowly-scoped rubric dimensions: `instruction_following`, `text_accuracy`, `visual_consistency`, and `layout_preservation`. Raw `overall_quality` is excluded from cross-model level comparisons. This subsection establishes why.

The instruction-not-followed control in Section 4.2.3 places Gemini at OQ = 1.00, GPT-4o at OQ = 2.94, and Qwen at OQ = 3.62 when no edit is applied. The 2.62-point spread on a five-point scale is the direct consequence of an under-specified prompt. The same stimuli, evaluated against the same prompt, produce non-comparable scores depending on which model is judging.

This scope ambiguity is itself a finding. It supplies worked-example evidence for why decomposed rubrics are the right design choice for VLM-based text edit evaluation, and it connects to a broader pattern in the LLM- and VLM-as-judge literature: holistic verdicts diverge across judge models in ways that decomposed verdicts do not (Prosa, Sotelo et al. 2025; matched-holistic study, Zhang 2025) (Note: check this), and global image-quality dimensions correlate poorly with human judgment in image editing (VTEdit-Bench, 2025) (Note: remove?). The noop control extends this pattern to text editing in images, in a controlled-stimulus setting where the cross-model disagreement cannot be explained by differential perceptual sensitivity, since the stimuli are identical and unedited. TextEditBench and WeEdit both decompose their evaluation rubrics; the noop divergence on `overall_quality` provides empirical justification for that design choice.

The narrow dimensions provide the contrast. Each narrow dimension has a defined scope that every model fills similarly, not always at score 1 on noop stimuli, since `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` legitimately remain near ceiling when the text content and layout are intact, but consistently across models. The noop `narrow_mean` spread across the three models is 0.41 points (Qwen 3.77, Gemini 4.00, GPT-4o 4.18), compared to 2.62 points for `overall_quality`. Defined scopes produce cross-model comparability even when constituent dimensions have legitimate ceiling effects; under-specified scopes do not. The `narrow_mean` baseline is high (rather than near 1) because text content is intact and layout is preserved in noop stimuli, but what matters for cross-model comparability is the between-model consistency, not the absolute level.

Two analytical choices follow. First, `narrow_mean` is the primary cross-model quality aggregate throughout Sections 4.5.2–4.5.5. Model rankings, between-model comparisons of absolute levels, and cross-model claims about per-dimension sensitivity are reported on `narrow_mean`. Second, `overall_quality` is reported alongside `narrow_mean` for completeness, its values appear as reference columns in the descriptive and per-dimension tables, but is interpreted only on its own terms, within a single model. The within-model analysis of `overall_quality`, including its rank-ordering value and its dominance by `visual_consistency`, is treated separately in Section 4.5.6.

Several patterns stand out immediately.

**`text_accuracy` is at ceiling for all models.** Mean scores of 4.81–4.99 and ceiling rates of 95–99.8% indicate that all three models consistently rate text content accuracy at 5 regardless of the degradation applied. This is expected and appropriate: the degradations in this study do not modify text content (no substitution of characters, no spelling errors), so text accuracy should remain 5 throughout. The consistency of this behaviour across models validates that the `text_accuracy` dimension is not confounding the other scores.

**`layout_preservation` shows near-ceiling behaviour.** Qwen (99.2% ceiling rate, mean 4.97) and GPT-4o (87.4% ceiling rate, mean 4.78) rate layout preservation as 5 in the vast majority of cases. This is structurally expected: the stimulus degradations all operate within the existing layout, text elements are scaled, recoloured, rotated, or otherwise modified, but the overall document structure is preserved. Gemini shows a somewhat lower ceiling rate (84.7%) and a floor rate of 11.7%, suggesting that Gemini applies the layout_preservation dimension more dynamically, in some cases penalising position offsets or rotations as layout changes. For Qwen and GPT-4o, `layout_preservation` essentially adds no discriminatory information in this experiment design.

**`instruction_following` shows the largest between-model variance.** Qwen's `instruction_following` mean of 2.80 with a floor rate of 35.2% indicates that Qwen frequently assigns score 1 on this dimension — yet the noop control analysis (Section 4.2.3) showed that Qwen also assigns score 1 to non-applied edits only 35% of the time overall (mean IF = 1.71), with some cases of score 3 and 5 given to unstimulated images. Gemini's `instruction_following` (mean 3.68, ceiling 65.6%) reflects its effective use of the dimension to penalise non-compliance with the instruction, consistent with the noop control result where Gemini universally scored `instruction_following` as 1.

**Model ranking on `narrow_mean`.** Using the cross-model comparable aggregate, the ranking is GPT-4o (4.62) > Gemini (4.25) > Qwen (4.18). All three means are high because `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` are near ceiling for all models, pulling the aggregate up. The discriminatory signal in `narrow_mean` comes primarily from `instruction_following` and `visual_consistency`, which do track degradation magnitude for image-level and some structural dimensions, as Section 4.5.4 will show. Unlike raw `overall_quality`, these `narrow_mean` levels are interpretable as cross-model comparisons.

Cronbach's alpha is approximately 0.76–0.78 for all three models, reflecting moderate but not high internal consistency. The rubric dimensions are neither fully redundant nor fully independent. Two structural patterns emerge from the intercorrelation matrix.

First, `visual_consistency` is the strongest single predictor of `overall_quality` across all models (ρ = 0.72–0.80). This is the dimension most directly measuring whether non-target properties of the image changed — that is, whether the degradation affected properties it should not have. The high correlation indicates that `overall_quality` is largely driven by `visual_consistency`, which in the context of this study means that a model's Experiment 2 holistic score is primarily tracking visible visual artefacts rather than instruction adherence per se. This pattern is taken up in Section 4.5.6.

Second, `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` are weakly correlated with `overall_quality` for most models. `layout_preservation` is almost always rated 5 (Section 4.5.2), so its correlation with `overall_quality` is attenuated. `text_accuracy` is at ceiling (Section 4.5.2), adding essentially no variance. For Gemini, `layout_preservation` correlates more strongly with OQ (ρ = 0.55) because Gemini uses the dimension more dynamically.

Third, `instruction_following` correlates with OQ most strongly for Gemini and GPT-4o (ρ = 0.66 and 0.77 respectively), but more weakly for Qwen (ρ = 0.38). This is consistent with the noop control result: for Gemini and GPT-4o, `instruction_following` meaningfully predicts the edit quality assessment; for Qwen, OQ is partly decoupled from instruction adherence.

### Colour offset: Complete insensitivity in Experiment 2 for Qwen

The most striking result in Experiment 2 is Qwen's `color_offset` `narrow_mean` of 5.00 (and OQ of 5.00) — every single colour-offset stimulus receives the maximum score from Qwen, regardless of magnitude. The distribution has zero variance: all 40 stimuli are scored 5 across all 10 magnitude tiers. Qwen assigns the maximum possible quality score under the edit judgment task to every colour-error stimulus it receives. This is a harder failure mode than low detection rate: it is a complete inability to penalise any colour deviation.

Gemini's `narrow_mean` for colour offset is 4.90 (OQ 4.65), and GPT-4o's is 4.81 (OQ 4.50). Both are near ceiling, and crucially both show significant Spearman correlations with magnitude for `narrow_mean` (Gemini ρ = −0.563***, GPT-4o ρ = −0.445**), unlike Qwen. This means Gemini and GPT-4o do detect increasing colour offset magnitudes in their aggregate score, even if the absolute scores remain high.

### Image-level degradations: Sensitivity attenuated by narrow dimensions

Blur and Gaussian noise produce the lowest `narrow_mean` values in Experiment 2: Gemini 3.04 (blur) and 3.29 (noise), Qwen 3.79 (blur) and 3.63 (noise), GPT-4o 3.94 (blur) and 4.25 (noise). Notably, these `narrow_mean` values are considerably higher than Gemini's raw OQ of 1.66 (blur) and 1.57 (noise). The reason is structural: when `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` remain near 5 even for blurred images (the text content and layout are unaffected by blur), their ceiling values pull up `narrow_mean` relative to OQ. The `narrow_mean` therefore provides a more measured signal than OQ for image-layer degradations, reflecting that only some rubric dimensions — `instruction_following` and `visual_consistency` — register blur and noise as failures.

Spearman correlations with magnitude for `narrow_mean` are significant for Qwen (−0.520** blur, −0.510*** noise), Gemini (−0.618*** blur, −0.660*** noise), and GPT-4o (−0.765*** noise). GPT-4o's blur shows no significant correlation (n/s, ρ = −0.123), consistent with its more lenient scoring on this dimension.

### JPEG compression: GPT-4o inflation persists in narrow_mean

GPT-4o assigns a mean `narrow_mean` of 4.95 to JPEG-compressed stimuli — the highest of any dimension, and above its overall `narrow_mean` of 4.62. Raw OQ is 4.85. Both metrics confirm the inflation pattern: GPT-4o does not register JPEG compression as an edit quality failure in either the holistic or the disaggregated rubric. This is examined further in Section 4.6.

Gemini's `narrow_mean` for JPEG compression is 4.64 (OQ 4.16), with a significant negative correlation for `narrow_mean` (ρ = −0.490**), indicating that higher compression magnitudes do depress Gemini's aggregate score even if absolute scores remain high.

### Typographic dimensions: OQ penalisation not fully preserved in narrow_mean

For `font_family`, `font_style`, and `font_weight`, Gemini assigns much lower OQ scores (2.97, 2.82, 3.89/3.67) than Qwen (4.28, 4.41, 4.19/3.96) or GPT-4o (4.08, 4.47, 4.48/4.58). However, in `narrow_mean` this gap is substantially attenuated: Gemini font_family NM = 4.07, Qwen = 4.17, GPT-4o = 4.52. Gemini font_style NM = 3.99, Qwen = 4.22, GPT-4o = 4.72. The narrowing happens because Gemini's harsh OQ judgments are primarily driven by `instruction_following` and `visual_consistency` — which are legitimate dimension-specific penalties — but `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` stay near ceiling and pull the aggregate up. The `narrow_mean` thus registers Gemini's typographic sensitivity via significant magnitude correlations (font_family ρ = −0.420***) rather than low absolute means.

For Qwen, `font_family` NM of 4.17 comes without a significant magnitude correlation (n/s, ρ = −0.070), whereas the raw OQ showed a significant ρ = −0.360**. This indicates that Qwen's within-OQ rank ordering of font substitutions (cross-class vs same-class) does not survive aggregation across the four narrow dimensions — the OQ correlation was capturing holistic variation that the individual dimensions do not reproduce.

## 4.5.5 Summary: Experiment 2 Findings Relative to RQ1

Experiment 2 replicates and in some cases amplifies the patterns found in Experiment 1. Qwen2.5-VL shows the most severe limitation: colour deviations receive near-maximum scores regardless of magnitude, layout preservation is always 5, and text accuracy is always 5, leaving only `visual_consistency` and `instruction_following` as potentially discriminatory dimensions — both of which show high ceiling rates. The benchmark-facing Experiment 2 is, for Qwen, less informative than the isolated perceptual test.

Gemini's Experiment 2 scoring is more functional as a quality measure. Its scores track magnitude more faithfully, the rubric dimensions are used more heterogeneously, and its `instruction_following` dimension provides real discriminatory power on most error types.

GPT-4o occupies an intermediate position, with moderate sensitivity on most detected dimensions but with low detection rates on font weight, font style, and spatial position.

On `narrow_mean`, the cross-model ranking is GPT-4o (4.62) > Gemini (4.25) > Qwen (4.18). All three means are high because `text_accuracy` and `layout_preservation` are near ceiling for all models, pulling the aggregate up. The discriminatory signal in `narrow_mean` comes primarily from `instruction_following` and `visual_consistency`, which do track degradation magnitude for image-level and some structural dimensions. Unlike raw `overall_quality`, these `narrow_mean` levels are interpretable as cross-model comparisons.

## 4.5.6 Within-Model Use of `overall_quality`

Section 4.5.1 established that raw `overall_quality` levels are not cross-model comparable, because each model resolves the dimension's under-specified scope with a different prior. This subsection examines what `overall_quality` does provide within a single model.

**Within-model rank analysis.** Despite the non-comparability of raw OQ levels across models, `overall_quality` retains useful within-model information. The rank ordering of stimuli within a single model's OQ scores is not affected by the cross-model scale offset, since the offset is a constant per model. The Spearman correlations between Experiment 1 similarity and Experiment 2 `overall_quality` reported in Table 4.12 and discussed in Section 4.6 are valid within-model analyses on this basis: they measure whether the model's Experiment 2 holistic judgment tracks its Experiment 1 perceptual signal, and the scale baseline does not enter the rank calculation.

The within-model OQ magnitude sensitivity data is similarly informative. Gemini's low OQ means for blur and Gaussian noise, Qwen's OQ ceiling effect on colour offset, and GPT-4o's elevated OQ on JPEG compression (Tables 4.11 and 4.13) are genuine within-model patterns and can be interpreted as such. What cannot be done is to compare Qwen's mean OQ of 4.05 on a given degradation against Gemini's mean OQ of 3.40 on the same degradation and conclude that Qwen rates the edit higher, since the baselines differ.

**`overall_quality` is dominated by `visual_consistency`.** The intercorrelation matrix (Table 4.10) shows that `visual_consistency` is the strongest predictor of OQ across all three models (ρ = 0.72–0.80). The holistic judgment is primarily tracking visible visual artefacts rather than instruction adherence. This is consequential for benchmark design: if the goal is to measure whether the edit instruction was followed, `overall_quality` is a less targeted signal than `instruction_following` or a defined aggregate over the narrow dimensions, and the dominance of `visual_consistency` in the intercorrelation matrix means that aggregating with OQ included risks double-counting visual artefacts and under-weighting instruction adherence.
